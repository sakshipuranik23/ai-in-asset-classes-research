Proxy Voting Optimisation

For decades, the industry has suffered from a "monoculture" of proxy voting where every fund followed the same two advisors. The result: identical votes across the market, no independent voice, and a governance system that rubber-stamps rather than scrutinises. AI makes independent, policy-grounded voting possible at machine scale.

NLP / Semantic Matching GenAI Justification Policy Vectorisation ML Cross-Reference Partially Deployed Implementation Readiness: 7/10
The Gap
Thousands of proxy resolutions arrive in a concentrated Proxy Season. Human-led independent analysis of every resolution is impossible at scale — so funds outsource their vote to two advisors who produce identical outcomes across the market.
AI Solution
A Resolution-to-Vote pipeline: NLP matches each resolution against the fund's Stewardship Code, ML cross-references company context, and GenAI drafts a unique justification — all grounded in the fund's own proprietary values, not a third party's.
Value Created
Every vote carries a defensible, policy-grounded rationale — satisfying regulators, differentiating the fund's stewardship posture, and eliminating the legal risk of blind proxy advisor delegation.
Strategic Framing — Fiduciary Autonomy
"For decades, the market has suffered from a monoculture of proxy voting where everyone followed the same two advisors. AI allows institutional investors to reclaim their Independent Voice — analysing every resolution through the unique lens of their own proprietary values, at the speed and scale of a machine. The vote becomes a strategic act, not a compliance checkbox."

Research Notes

⚠ AI Challenge — The Contradictory Policies Problem
When Pro-Growth and Net Zero Collide
A fund with both a Pro-Growth mandate and a Net Zero commitment faces an inherent tension when voting on a resolution that links executive pay to ESG targets — because tying executive pay to carbon reduction goals may constrain short-term profitability. The AI cannot resolve this conflict without a hierarchical priority list explicitly defined by the fund. If Net Zero ranks above Pro-Growth, the AI votes For. If the hierarchy is reversed, it votes Against. The danger is that funds often have not explicitly ranked their own policies — they exist in parallel, each absolute in its own section of the Stewardship Handbook. This is not a technical problem. It is a governance design problem that must be resolved by humans before the AI can operate coherently. The system's most valuable contribution may be surfacing these latent policy contradictions before proxy season, forcing the fund to articulate a priority ordering it has never had to make explicit before.
⚠ Regulatory Risk — The Rubber Stamp Problem
Following ISS is Not Independent Analysis
Regulators in the UK (FCA), EU (ESMA), and increasingly SEBI are scrutinising whether asset managers are exercising genuine independent judgment in proxy voting or simply delegating to ISS and Glass Lewis and calling it stewardship. A fund that cannot demonstrate why its votes differ from the proxy advisor's recommendations — or worse, a fund whose votes are statistically indistinguishable from ISS recommendations — is at material risk of regulatory challenge for failing its fiduciary duty. The Conflict Queue in the Stewardship Dashboard is the direct regulatory defence: it is documented evidence that the fund actively reviewed every case where AI and proxy advisor disagreed, considered both perspectives, and made an independent decision. That audit trail is the difference between stewardship and rubber-stamping.
Technical Architecture
Policy VectoriserThe fund's 50-page Stewardship Code is converted into mathematical intent vectors using an embedding model — each policy clause becomes a point in semantic space that resolution text can be compared against
Semantic MatchingA resolution about "Supply Chain Transparency" is matched against the "Carbon Emissions" policy clause via cosine similarity — capturing conceptual overlap that keyword search entirely misses
Company Context LayerML regression cross-references each resolution with the company's financial health, historical ESG scores, and voting history — a pay resolution at an underperforming company carries a different weight than the same resolution at an outperformer
GenAI JustificationEach vote generates a unique 2–3 sentence rationale citing the specific policy clause, the resolution's key terms, and the company context that drove the decision — no two justifications are identical
